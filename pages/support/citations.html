Source Quality & Client Verifiability

Your client is a real organization with a CFO, a legal team, and a compliance officer. They paid for your engagement report. The first thing any of them will do with a finding that matters is try to verify it independently. They’ll click your citation. They’ll search the URL. They’ll forward your report to their auditor.

If they can’t find your source — or if the source they find can’t be trusted as a stable, authoritative reference — your finding is worthless to them. Worse: it’s worthless to you, because the next contract they sign won’t be with your firm.

The 10-citation requirement isn’t bureaucratic hoop-jumping. It’s the floor of professional credibility.

The Standard: Every citation toward your 10-source minimum must pass one test: Can your client verify this without your help?

Not “would they bother?” — can they. If a reasonable person at the client organization can pull up your source on their own, read it, and confirm your claim, the citation counts. If they can’t, it doesn’t — no matter how credible the source is in absolute terms.
Why This Standard

In a real engagement, your firm bills the client for analyst time spent on research. A finding supported by a Medium post or a tutorial site doesn’t survive client review. A finding supported by NIST SP 800-63B does. The skill this course builds is the skill of credible research — and that skill is gated entirely on whether you can identify, find, and cite sources your client can verify.

You may use any source as a starting point for your own understanding. The question is what you cite.

“Why allow for mediocrity that wouldn’t fly in the real world?”

Deduction: −5% per missing citation (10 required). Capped at −50%. This requirement is non-negotiable.
What Counts

These sources pass the client-verifiability test. They have stable URLs, institutional authority, and don’t disappear:

  • Standards bodies: NIST publications (csrc.nist.gov, pages.nist.gov), RFCs (rfc-editor.org), CISA advisories
  • Industry standards: OWASP cheat sheets, MITRE ATT&CK and CVE databases, CIS Benchmarks
  • Tool documentation: Official Hashcat (hashcat.net), John the Ripper (openwall.com), OpenSSL, Wireshark User’s Guide and Display Filter Reference, Nmap reference manual
  • System documentation: Linux man pages from authoritative mirrors (man7.org, kernel.org, linux.die.net), Microsoft Learn (learn.microsoft.com) for Microsoft topics, AWS docs for AWS topics
  • Academic: Peer-reviewed papers with full citations (DOI when available)
  • Primary government and standards body publications: any source whose authority is institutional rather than editorial
What Does NOT Count

These sources may help you learn, but they won’t survive client review. They don’t count toward your 10:

  • Wikipedia (any article) — content shifts; today’s article isn’t tomorrow’s
  • Textbooks, including this course’s textbook (Conklin & White) — see the textbook clause below
  • Blog posts (vendor-published or independent) — can disappear, paywall, or be rewritten
  • Tutorial sites — GeeksforGeeks, TutorialsPoint, W3Schools, and similar
  • Forum threads — Reddit, Stack Overflow, Stack Exchange
  • YouTube videos and other video content
  • AI chatbot responses
  • Course Canvas pages (the assignment is not a source for itself)
  • Generic “About” or marketing pages from security vendors
  • Aggregator sites that summarize without primary attribution
The Textbook Clause: Treat your textbook the way you’d treat Wikipedia: a useful starting point, never the citation.

Three reasons it never makes it to the report:

It looks unprofessional. Handing a client a finding backed by a textbook — even a good one — signals that you couldn’t find a real source. Real engagements cite specifications, standards, and vendor documentation. Real consultants do not cite textbooks. Your client will notice.

It’s out of date. Security textbooks lag the field by 3–5 years between writing and publication. By the time a vulnerability or technique shows up in a textbook chapter, NIST has published updates, vendors have issued advisories, and the specifics have moved. The textbook describes the past. Your client is paying you to address the present.

Your client can’t verify it. They don’t own a copy of Conklin & White. They can’t flip to page 487. Even if your citation is accurate, gating the truth behind a book they don’t have means it might as well not exist.

The textbook tells you what to learn. You find the source. When the textbook discusses a topic you want to cite — NTLM authentication, certificate revocation, ARP poisoning, whatever — your job is to go find the primary source on your own. Search Microsoft Learn for the protocol spec. Search NIST for the standard. Search OWASP for the cheat sheet. The textbook gave you the vocabulary; the research is yours to do. This applies regardless of whether the textbook is required, recommended, or supplementary.
The Research Pattern

The most common pattern of weak sourcing is starting on a blog, Wikipedia, or textbook, finding a useful claim, and citing the blog, Wikipedia, or textbook. The professional pattern looks different:

  • Find a useful claim on whatever source you’re reading
  • Identify the primary source for that claim — either by following a citation if one exists, or by searching for it yourself (NIST, vendor docs, RFCs, etc.)
  • Read the primary source and verify it actually says what you thought
  • Cite the primary source — not the article or chapter that led you to it

Worked example — NTLM salting (citation exists): You read on a security blog that NTLM has no salting. The blog cites no source, but the claim is verifiable. Open the Microsoft NTLM specification on Microsoft Learn (learn.microsoft.com/en-us/openspecs/windows_protocols/ms-nlmp), confirm the no-salt design, cite the Microsoft specification.

Worked example — rockyou.txt (citation chain): You read on Wikipedia that the rockyou.txt wordlist contains 14 million leaked passwords from a 2009 breach. Wikipedia’s citation is to a Computerworld article. Click through, read the Computerworld article, cite Computerworld — or even better, find the original breach disclosure the article references.

Worked example — Textbook concept (no citation to chase): You read in Chapter 5 that AES uses substitution-permutation networks. The textbook doesn’t cite a source. This is normal for this textbook. Your job: search NIST for the AES specification (FIPS 197), read it, cite it. The textbook gave you the concept and the vocabulary; finding the authoritative source is the research skill you’re building.

How to Cite — Using Microsoft Word

Use Word’s built-in citation tools. Do not type references manually — Word automates formatting and bibliography generation.

  • Place your cursor where the citation belongs in the text
  • Go to References → Insert Citation → Add New Source
  • Fill in source details (author, title, URL, date accessed)
  • At document end: References → Bibliography → Insert Bibliography
  • Before submitting: Select All (Ctrl+A) then Update Fields (F9)

Style: APA format. Consistency matters.

Pro Tip: Cite as you work. Keep a running list of sources as you move through each lab. When you look something up, add it immediately. Reconstructing your sources after the fact is painful and usually incomplete.
