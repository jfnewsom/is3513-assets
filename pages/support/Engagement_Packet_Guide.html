Two Audiences, One Document, Every Module

Every module ends with an Engagement Packet — the work product that wraps up your client engagement. A real security engagement produces two distinct artifacts: documentation that stays inside the firm, and a deliverable that goes to the client. Your Engagement Packet mirrors that reality.

The audience rule

Part I is for us. Part II is for them. The client never sees Part I — your timesheet, your runbook, your reflection on the engagement. That’s NEXUS internal documentation. The client only sees Part II — the Client Deliverable. Anything you’d be embarrassed to show the client (“I got stuck for two hours on this nmap flag,” “ChatGPT helped me figure out what the error meant”) belongs in Part I, not Part II. Keep the audience boundary clean and your writing becomes a lot easier.

Part I: The Internal Packet

Part I is what NEXUS keeps in the engagement file. Three components, all internal:

A. NEXUS Timesheet

Audience: NEXUS leadership / billing.

Where did your hours go? Hours broken out by unit, split between internal training (Units 1–2, non-billable) and client billable (Unit 3 fieldwork). Justify any unusually high or low hour count with a one-line note (“spent 4 hours debugging tool installation”).

If you logged hours honestly as you went, this is a 10-minute fill-in. If you’re reconstructing it the night before, it’ll show — your scan dates, screenshot timestamps, and command history won’t line up with your claimed hours. The template includes a pre-built timesheet table; just fill in the cells.

✓ Good Example — Module 1 Timesheet

A Module 1 (Brazos Financial) timesheet that gives leadership exactly what they need to bill the client and assess the engagement:

WEEK 1 — Internal Training (4.0 hours)
• Kali VM installation, hostname configured to kali-abc123 ............... 1.5 hr
• VirtualBox network configuration (host-only adapter for engagement) .... 0.5 hr
• Lab 1.1 environment verification (uname -a, ip a, screenshot capture) .. 1.0 hr
• Documentation setup (screenshot folder structure, runbook scaffolding) . 1.0 hr

WEEK 2 — Internal Training (3.5 hours)
• nmap deep dive: read man page, tested -sS / -sV / -O flags ............. 1.5 hr
• dirb / nikto / enum4linux: practice runs against scanme.nmap.org ....... 1.0 hr
• searchsploit workflow: paired CVE lookup with version output .......... 0.5 hr
• Tool inventory documentation for runbook ............................. 0.5 hr

WEEK 3 — Client Billable (6.0 hours)
• Brazos Financial: full TCP scan, top-1000 ports ........................ 0.5 hr
• Service version detection on open ports (-sV -sC --version-intensity 5) 1.0 hr
• Web reconnaissance: dirb, nikto, manual inspection .................... 1.5 hr
• SMB enumeration: enum4linux against discovered host ................... 0.5 hr
• Vulnerability research: searchsploit + CVE lookups on discovered versions 1.5 hr
• Client Deliverable: executive summary + 3 finding writeups + risk analysis 1.0 hr

ENGAGEMENT TOTAL: 13.5 hours  |  Internal: 7.5 hr  |  Billable: 6.0 hr
Why this works:
  • Every line ties to a specific activity, command, or output
  • Times are realistic and reflect actual effort — not all 30-min round numbers
  • Internal vs. billable categories are clearly distinguished
  • A billing manager could justify the 6 client-billable hours from the listed activities
  • The total aligns with what your screenshot timestamps and command history would show
✗ Bad Example — Vague, Round-Number Timesheet

A timesheet that fails the basic "could a manager bill the client from this" test:

Unit 1 (3 hours)
- Did the lab stuff
- Set up VM

Unit 2 (2 hours)
- Practiced with tools
- Took some screenshots

Unit 3 (4 hours)
- Scanned the client
- Wrote report

Total: 9 hours
Why this fails:
  • "Did the lab stuff" tells leadership nothing — there's no way to justify the hours to a client
  • No breakdown by specific tools or commands
  • Times are suspiciously round (3, 2, 4) — real engagement work isn't billed in whole-hour chunks
  • No internal vs. billable distinction — leadership can't tell which hours go on the client invoice
  • The total (9 hr) is implausibly low for a three-unit engagement — either you didn't do the work or didn't track it
  • Would be rejected by any real consulting firm before reaching the client
B. Internal Runbook

Audience: The next analyst who has to do this engagement next month.

If a colleague picked up this client next quarter, could they reproduce what you did from your runbook alone? Environment setup, tools you used (and which versions), the exact commands you ran, what the expected output looks like, and where you got stuck and how you got unstuck. This is the document that prevents the firm from re-learning the same things every engagement.

Good runbook test: screenshots are paired with the command that produced them. Commands are in code blocks, not in prose. There’s enough context that a reader who wasn’t there can tell what was working and what wasn’t.

Foundation Labs 1–2 of each module produce most of the runbook material. Lab 3 (the client engagement) adds the engagement-specific portion.

✓ Good Example — nmap Service Detection Runbook Entry

A runbook entry the next analyst can actually use without you. From a Module 1 engagement, documenting the nmap service detection pass:

NMAP SERVICE VERSION DETECTION
────────────────────────────────────────

Purpose: Identify running services and their specific versions on target hosts.
Version info is the bridge from "this port is open" to "this port has CVE-XXXX-XXXXX."

Recommended flags:
  -sV                       Service version detection
  -sC                       Default scripts (safe recon — banner grabs, etc.)
  --version-intensity 5     Thorough probing (1–9 scale, 5 = balanced)
  -oN output.txt            Save to text file (human-readable)

Full engagement command (as run on Brazos):
  nmap -sV -sC --version-intensity 5 -oN brazos-svc.txt 10.0.0.0/24

Sample output excerpt:
  PORT      STATE  SERVICE    VERSION
  22/tcp    open   ssh        OpenSSH 8.9p1 Ubuntu 3
  80/tcp    open   http       Apache httpd 2.4.29        ← flag this version
  3306/tcp  open   mysql      MySQL 5.7.42               ← EOL, research CVEs

Next steps after scan:
  1. Record every service version in your findings doc
  2. Search NVD/CVE databases for each version
  3. Flag any version that's EOL or has known CVEs
  4. Cross-reference against searchsploit for available exploits

Pitfalls observed during this engagement:
  • --version-intensity 9 took ~3x longer than 5 and surfaced no extra info
    on this target — stick with 5 unless you have a specific reason
Why this works:
  • Purpose is stated in plain language — a junior analyst reading this knows why they're running it, not just what to type
  • Every flag is explained — no magic incantations
  • Sample output is annotated — the reader knows what to look at and why
  • "Next steps" tells the reader what to do with the output, not just how to get it
  • "Pitfalls observed" captures hard-won knowledge that would otherwise be lost
✗ Bad Example — Journal-Style Runbook Entry

A runbook entry that's technically a record of what you did, but won't help anyone reproduce it:

nmap

I used nmap to scan the client. The command I used was nmap -sV -sC and it found
some open ports including 22, 80, and 3306. I saved the output to a file.

Then I looked up the versions on Google and found some vulnerabilities.
Why this fails:
  • No target specified — the next analyst can't tell what subnet you scanned
  • Command is incomplete — no -oN flag, no --version-intensity, no scope
  • "Some open ports" and "some vulnerabilities" are useless — specifics are the whole point of a runbook
  • "Looked up the versions on Google" gives no path the next analyst can follow — NVD? CVE Details? searchsploit?
  • Reads like a journal entry, not professional documentation
  • If your colleague picked up Brazos next quarter, this runbook would help them nothing
C. Analyst Reflection

Audience: You + your instructor. This is the section where the learning lives.

Three prompts to answer:

  • Timesheet Self-Audit. Looking at your timesheet for this engagement: was there a task or section that took disproportionately long compared to the others? Describe what made it slow — unfamiliar tool, unclear instructions, a tangent you went down, troubleshooting an error — and what you’d do differently next time to handle a similar task more efficiently. Then total your hours across the three weeks: if Maya Rodriguez reviewed this timesheet alongside the deliverable you’re submitting, what conclusion would she draw about your efficiency on this engagement? Be candid — this is a professional self-audit, not a grade defense.
  • Professional Reflection. What worked? What didn’t? If you ran this engagement again next semester with what you know now, what would you do differently? Be specific — “I’d communicate findings to the client earlier” is more useful than “I’d be more organized.”
  • AI Methodology Reflection. Where did AI help on this engagement? How did you verify what it told you? And honestly: did you really need it, or were you using it as a shortcut? (See the dedicated AI reflection section below.)

This section is internal. The client never reads it. Write candidly. “I got stuck on tool X for two hours” is fine here. “I learned that the client’s naming convention was confusing” is fine here. The point of the section is honest reflection, not polish.

Part II: The Client Deliverable

Part II is the document you hand the client. The structure follows professional penetration-test reporting conventions — the client should be able to read the executive summary alone and know what to do.

The Word template defines the exact section order and required headings. Use the template; don’t invent your own structure. At a high level, Part II contains:

  • Cover Page — client name, engagement title, your name (as NEXUS analyst), date
  • Executive Summary — one page maximum, board-readable, what we found and what to do
  • Methodology — brief overview of how the engagement was conducted; client doesn’t need command-by-command detail (that’s in your runbook)
  • Findings — organized by severity, highest first; each finding has a finding number, title, description, evidence (screenshot), and impact
  • Risk Analysis — likelihood × impact for each finding
  • Recommendations — prioritized; what to do Monday vs. what to plan for the next budget cycle
  • Appendix — references, supporting evidence, scrubbed scan output

This section is client-facing. Write like a consultant. No first-person. No “I was confused.” No AI references. No reflections. Recommendations-forward, evidence-backed, professional throughout.

✓ Good Example — Medium-Severity Finding (Apache CVE)

A Medium-severity finding from a Module 1 Brazos Financial engagement. Note the structure: number, title, severity, description, evidence, impact, recommendation:

FINDING 003 — Outdated Apache HTTP Server Exposes Multiple CVEs
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Severity:       Medium
Affected Host:  brazos-external.local (10.0.0.50)
Affected Port:  80/tcp

Description:
The Brazos Financial external web server is running Apache HTTP Server 2.4.29,
released March 2018. Versions of Apache prior to 2.4.55 are affected by multiple
publicly disclosed vulnerabilities, including CVE-2022-31813 (mod_proxy header
mishandling) and CVE-2022-22720 (HTTP request smuggling). Exploit code for several
of these issues is publicly available.

Evidence:
[See Figure 7 — nmap service-detection output showing "Apache httpd 2.4.29".]
[See Appendix A.3 for the full nmap output excerpt.]

Impact:
An attacker who successfully exploits one of the disclosed Apache vulnerabilities
could intercept or manipulate HTTP traffic to the Brazos external site, potentially
exposing customer session data or enabling further attack chaining into Brazos's
internal network.

Recommendation:
Upgrade Apache HTTP Server to version 2.4.58 or later. Apache project provides
backported security fixes for the 2.4.x line. Estimated effort: low (1–2 hours
for a standard deployment). Priority: complete within 30 days.

Reference: Apache Security Advisories — https://httpd.apache.org/security/vulnerabilities_24.html
Why this works:
  • Finding has a number (003) so the executive summary and risk table can reference it
  • Severity is stated up front — a client reading only the first line knows the urgency
  • Description names specific CVEs and version cutoffs, not vague "outdated software"
  • Impact is in business terms ("customer session data," "further attack chaining") not technical jargon
  • Recommendation includes effort estimate AND priority window — actionable on Monday
  • Evidence references existing figures rather than embedding a 30-line scan output in the body
✗ Bad Example — Vague, Unactionable Finding

A finding that's technically present but won't help the client decide what to do:

FINDING: Apache is old.

The Apache web server is running an old version. This is bad because old software
has vulnerabilities. The version is 2.4.29.

I recommend updating it.
Why this fails:
  • No severity rating — client can't prioritize against other findings
  • No finding number — can't be referenced in the executive summary or risk analysis
  • "Old version" and "vulnerabilities" are not specific — which version cutoff? Which CVEs?
  • "This is bad" is not an impact statement — what could an attacker actually do?
  • "Update it" is not a recommendation — update to what? On what timeline? At what cost?
  • No evidence reference — client has to take your word for it
  • If the client's IT team has to triage this against 20 other findings, they have no way to decide whether this is finding #1 or finding #15
The AI Methodology Reflection

Every Engagement Packet includes a brief AI Methodology Reflection in Part I.C. The prompt is simple:

“Where did AI help you on this engagement, how did you verify what it told you, and did you really need it?”

There’s no minimum word count — just answer the question honestly. “I didn’t use AI on this engagement (N/A)” is a perfectly acceptable answer if it’s true. But it had better be true — don’t claim N/A if your runbook is full of AI-generated explanations.

A good reflection covers:

  • What you asked AI for — specific tasks, not “it helped me understand things”
  • How you verified the answer — man page, official docs, NIST publication, your own testing
  • What you learned that you didn’t know before — the actual knowledge transfer
  • An honest take on whether you needed AI — or whether you used it because it was faster than reading the documentation

The reflection isn’t a gotcha. Using AI well is a skill. Using AI poorly is a tell. The reflection lets you show me which one you’re doing. See the GenAI Policy page for the full framework.

✓ Authentic Reflection
Specific, verified, honest — “I asked Claude to explain what the -sS flag does in nmap. The response said it was a SYN-scan that doesn’t complete the TCP handshake. I verified this against the nmap man page (man nmap, lines 124–138) and confirmed it. I could have read the man page first — in hindsight that would have been faster, since I had to read it anyway to verify. For Module 2 I’m going to try man page first, AI second.”
✗ Performative Reflection
Generic, unverified, performative — “I used AI to help me understand the concepts in this engagement. It was very helpful for explaining things and helped me to learn the material. I verified its responses by cross-checking with the textbook and online sources. AI is a powerful tool that I will continue to use in future engagements as I develop my skills as a security analyst.”
What Gets Penalized

Documentation deductions are calibrated to the severity of each failure in a real engagement. Combined deductions cap at −100%.

  • −5% per missing screenshot below the 20-screenshot minimum (Modules 1–4). Module 5’s floor is set by the compressed lab structure and published in the Module 5 lab body. A screenshot without your VM hostname (kali-abc123) visible counts as missing.
  • −10% per missing reference below the 10-reference minimum. References must meet the source quality standard — see the Citations page. Wikipedia, the textbook, and tutorial sites do not count.
  • −20% if the Appendix is missing. The appendix is the audit trail — without it, your findings can’t be verified.
  • −10% if the Executive Summary exceeds one page. The one-page limit is a hard limit, not a guideline.
  • −5% if the Cover Page is missing.
  • −5% if the Table of Contents is missing or hand-typed (not generated from heading styles).
  • −5% if Word fields are not updated — visible “Error! Reference source not found” placeholders or stale figure numbers. Run Ctrl+A → F9 before submitting.

These penalties are pre-curve and transparent. If you turn in 17 screenshots, that’s −15%. If you turn in 6 quality references, that’s −40%. No subjectivity, no negotiation, no surprises. The deductions exist as a deterrent — if you’re doing the engagement work in good faith, you’ll rarely interact with them.

Word Automation Quick Reference

Don’t hand-format. Word can generate everything you need from your structure — use it. The template is set up to support all of these:

  • Table of Contents: References tab → Insert Table of Contents (uses your heading styles)
  • Bibliography: References tab → Insert Citation, then → Bibliography. Auto-generates from your inserted citations.
  • Figure captions: References tab → Insert Caption (auto-numbers screenshots as Figure 1, Figure 2, etc.)
  • Cross-references: References tab → Cross-reference (live links to figure numbers, won’t break if you reorder)
  • Update everything before submission: Ctrl+A to select all, then F9 to update all fields. Do this last.
