Section A: NEXUS Timesheet

Audience: NEXUS Management  •  Purpose: Billing Justification

Consulting firms bill clients for time. Every hour must be justified. Generic entries like “did lab work” won’t fly — describe what you actually did.

Billing Categories:

  • Week 1 — Internal Training: Environment setup, initial tool learning, configuration
  • Week 2 — Internal Training: Tool exploration, technique practice, methodology development
  • Week 3 — Client Billable: Engagement execution, analysis, report writing

Example (Module 1): Week 1: Kali VM installation (1.0 hr), network adapter config (0.5 hr), snapshot setup (0.5 hr), tool inventory (1.0 hr), recon methodology review (1.0 hr) = 4.0 hrs internal. Week 3: Brazos external recon (2.0 hr), subdomain enumeration (1.0 hr), findings analysis (1.0 hr), report drafting (1.5 hr) = 5.5 hrs billable.

Section B: Internal Runbook

Audience: NEXUS Team  •  Purpose: Knowledge Transfer

Create methodology documentation so another analyst could reproduce your work. “Here’s how to do X for the next analyst.” When you leave, your methodology stays.

What to Include:

  • From Week 1: Environment setup steps, tool installation procedures, configuration documentation, screenshots of key steps
  • From Week 2: Tool usage documentation, command syntax with explanations, technique workflows, output interpretation guides
  • From Week 3: Engagement workflow, lessons learned, what worked and what didn’t, recommendations for next time

The Reproducibility Test: Could a junior analyst who wasn’t in this class follow your runbook and achieve the same results? If the answer is no, add more detail. Pro tip: If you took good screenshots in Labs X.1 and X.2, your runbook is 60–70% done before Week 3 starts. Just compile and polish.

Section C: Client Report

Audience: Fictional Client Contact  •  Purpose: Professional Deliverable

Write the report you would hand to a real client. This is what Ray Jimenez, Jennifer Walsh, Amanda Nguyen, or Bobby Alamo would actually read.

Required Sections:

  • 1. Executive Summary (1 page max) — High-level overview for non-technical readers. What did you find? What’s the risk? What should they do? No jargon. A CEO should understand this.
  • 2. Scope and Methodology — What systems were tested? What was out of scope? What approach did you take?
  • 3. Findings — Each finding needs: description, evidence (screenshots), severity, and business impact.
  • 4. Risk Analysis — What’s the actual risk to the business? Likelihood, impact, and business context. Not everything high-severity is high-priority.
  • 5. Recommendations — Actionable, prioritized, realistic. Quick wins and long-term improvements. Reference industry standards (CIS, NIST).
Writing: Do This
Write in third person, past tense
Use clear, professional language
Explain technical terms
Support all claims with evidence
Proofread for grammar and spelling
Writing: Avoid This
First person (“I ran nmap…”)
Unexplained jargon
Claims without screenshots
Copy-pasted tool output without analysis
Casual language or slang
Pre-Submission Checklist
  • File saved as .docx — not PDF, not Pages, not Google Docs export
  • All three sections present: Timesheet, Runbook, Client Report
  • 10+ citations with auto-generated bibliography (References → Bibliography)
  • All screenshots show VM name (kali-lastname-studentID visible)
  • Screenshot filenames follow ##-description.png convention
  • Executive summary is non-technical and under one page
  • Each finding includes severity, evidence, and business impact
  • Recommendations are actionable and reference industry standards
  • Document spellchecked and proofread
