1. Try AI First (For Syntax & Concepts)

If your question is “what does this flag do?” or “why am I getting this error?” or “what does this concept mean?” — ask an AI tool first. ChatGPT, Claude, and Copilot are excellent for syntax, error messages, and concept explanations. You’ll usually get an answer in seconds.

Verify what AI tells you against tool documentation, man pages, or the textbook. See the GenAI Policy for what AI can and can’t help with.

AI is best for:

  • Command syntax and flag explanations
  • Decoding cryptic error messages
  • Concept clarification (“explain ARP poisoning like I’m 12”)
  • Proofreading your writing

AI is NOT best for: course-specific questions (“do I need to cite this?”), policy questions (“can I get an extension?”), or anything that depends on context AI can’t see. Those go to Discord or email.

2. Ask on Discord (Fastest Response from Humans)

When AI can’t help — or when you need a human who knows the course — Discord is the fastest path. Classmates, course alumni, and the instructor monitor the channels; questions usually get answered within a few hours.

Pick the right channel:

  • #module-1-help through #module-5-help — lab technical questions for that specific module
  • #course-questions — policy, deadlines, grading questions
  • #technical-questions — general technical questions not tied to a specific module
  • #tools-and-resources — sharing useful tools, cheat sheets, finds

When asking questions: Describe what you’re trying to do. Share the error message in a code block. Include a screenshot if relevant — but use a real screen capture, not a phone photo of your monitor. Phone photos look unprofessional and are hard to read. Every OS has built-in screen-snipping tools (Windows: Win+Shift+S; macOS: Cmd+Shift+4); if you don’t know how to use them, AI does and will walk you through it. You need to know how to make clean screen captures anyway — you’ll use them in every Engagement Packet. Bring the same professional respect to your colleagues that you do to your clients.

Be specific — “it doesn’t work” doesn’t help us help you.

Post in public channels — not DMs. The whole point of the Discord server is peer learning. If you have a question, other students probably have it too. Asking in public means three things happen: you might get an answer from a classmate or alumnus faster than from me; everyone else with the same question gets the answer too; and the answer stays searchable for the next person who hits the same problem.

I check Discord frequently during the day, less so at night — I’m not a 24/7 lurker. If you post at 2 a.m., expect that I might not see it until the next afternoon. Give classmates a couple of hours to answer before DMing me directly. Only DM me if you’ve already posted publicly and didn’t get a usable response.

4. Email the Instructor (Private Matters Only)

Email is the only channel for anything personal: grade questions, accommodations, extension requests, personal circumstances, or anything tied to your specific situation.

Use your UTSA email — not a personal Gmail/Yahoo/Hotmail account. If you email me from a personal account, the only response you’ll get is “Please resend this email from your student account.” FERPA only allows me to discuss personal matters over UTSA-owned services — I cannot send an email about your record to a personal address. Emails on the UTSA server are also archived, which protects both of us.

Help me help you. Lead every email with this information:

Subject: IS3513 §[section] — [brief topic]
Body opener: My name is [name], abc123, IS3513 §[section].

I teach multiple courses with multiple sections, and emails that make me dig through five rosters to figure out who you are and what class you’re in get answered slower. If you’re asking for something — an extension, an exception, a recommendation — I’m also less inclined to grant it when the request shows up without context. The two seconds it takes to add this information buys you a faster, friendlier response.

Response time: Within 24 hours Mon–Fri. Weekend or holiday emails: next business day.

5. Weekly Drop-In Zoom (Open Office Hours)

Once a week I host an open Zoom for anyone who wants to ask something live. It’s not course-specific and it’s not private — think of it as walk-in office hours over Zoom. No appointment needed; just show up.

When: Tuesdays at 6:00 p.m. CT, every week of the term — starting the first week of classes.

How it works: I open the Zoom room at the scheduled time. If no one joins in the first five minutes, I close the room and move on with my day. If you show up, we talk.

Not recorded. Nothing is posted afterward. This is a real-time channel only.

Personal/FERPA-touching questions still belong in email, not the open Zoom — anyone could be in the room.
